# Writing in your voice

The site is opinionated about *not* sounding corporate. Here are the
guidelines so it stays consistent as you add content.

## The voice in three lines

1. **Like you're texting a smart friend.** Low ceremony, no jargon.
2. **Builder-first.** You ship things. You don't "leverage solutions."
3. **Honest > impressive.** "It was hard and I learned a lot" beats
   "I architected a robust solution."

## Words to use

- "I built", "I made", "I shipped"
- "weird", "small", "fun", "useful"
- "I'm into…", "I've been messing with…", "I learned…"
- "honestly", "actually", "basically" (sparingly)
- contractions ("I'm", "don't", "won't")

## Words to avoid

- "Passionate" — overused
- "Leverage" — corporate
- "Solutions" — meaningless
- "Robust" — empty word
- "Crafted" / "curated" — try-hard
- "Just" (as in "I just want to…") — undersells
- "Stack" used as a verb
- Emoji headers in case studies (a couple inline is fine if natural)

## Hero tagline patterns that work

The current placeholder: "i build small things on the internet."

Other patterns to riff on:

- "small apps. shipped on weekends."
- "a hobbyist builder, making things i wanted to exist."
- "i make weird tools. some of them work."
- "[your handle], building in public."
- "i ship things. claude helps."

Whichever you pick, keep it **under 8 words** and **lowercase**. The
lowercase is part of the vibe.

## About-section structure

The about page is intentionally short. Three blocks:

1. **Who you are.** One paragraph, casual.
2. **What you're working on.** A pointer to projects + now.
3. **How you work.** 3–5 bullets that say something real about you.

If you find yourself writing more than 250 words on the about page,
delete the most "professional" sounding ones.

## Project case study structure

The template uses six sections. Skip any that don't apply, but keep
the order:

1. **the problem** — why does this thing need to exist?
2. **what i built** — the plain-English description
3. **how it works** — the interesting decisions
4. **tech & tradeoffs** — short, scannable
5. **what i learned** — the honest reflection
6. **try it** — clear CTA

The biggest mistake people make in case studies is talking about
themselves instead of the *thing*. Lead with the work, let the
personality show through how you describe it.
